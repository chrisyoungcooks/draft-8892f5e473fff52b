This Chocolate Bar Became My Hardest Recipe

Working Draft

— The Hook —

1

TIGHT SHOT — the hands of a young chef unwrapping a Nestlé Aero bar. The foil peels back. The chocolate is revealed. (Same actor as shot 2.)

CHRIS (V.O.)

This chocolate bar was the inspiration for the most difficult dish I have ever developed. It's a Nestlé Aero bar — a chocolate bar filled with tiny bubbles of laughing gas.

2

WIDER SHOT — a young chef (early 20s, played by an actor — possibly Chris's son Jack) in his whites with a backpack, walking into the front door of the Fat Duck.

CHRIS (V.O.)

As a young American chef in England, I'd never had one before. But after I did, I wanted to create my version of it at The Fat Duck.

3

VERY TIGHT MACRO SHOT — the cross-section of an Aero bar. The tiny bubbles visible inside the chocolate.

CHRIS (V.O.)

But those tiny bubbles wouldn't do. They needed to be bigger!

4

CUT TO — present-day CHRIS (50, in casual clothes) walking out of the Fat Duck's front door, balloon in hand. Walk-and-talk with the camera.

CHRIS

The bubbles in the chocolate are just like this balloon — gas trapped inside thin walls. And like this balloon, to make them bigger, we need to let it go.

He releases the balloon.

5

CONTINUOUS WITH SHOT 4 — the camera rises with the balloon. The High Road falls away. Bray shrinks below. VFX telemetry data appears on screen: altitude, atmospheric pressure (outside balloon), internal pressure (inside balloon), temperature. The balloon is visibly expanding as pressure drops. Landmarks are called out with on-screen text as they come into view — the Thames, Windsor Castle, London in the distance. Drone footage transitions to AI-generated footage above ~1,000-2,000 feet as the camera climbs higher and higher. The telemetry keeps ticking. The balloon keeps growing. The camera keeps rising until — it dollies backward, pulling away from the balloon and through an airplane window — CONTINUOUS INTO SHOT 6.

6

CHRIS is seated in an airplane cabin at cruising altitude.

CHRIS

I didn't actually make chocolate in the stratosphere. I hacked our vacuum sealer to do the job.

7

B-ROLL — foams expanding inside a vacuum box. Pressure gauge dropping. Whipped cream and whipped chocolate side by side, both expanding.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Whether whipped cream or whipped chocolate, a foam under vacuum will expand like a rising balloon as the crushing weight of the atmosphere is removed.

8

CHRIS in the airplane cabin.

CHRIS

The vacuum chamber is acting like the fuselage of this airplane. Keeping the pressures different inside and outside. The problem for the foams under vacuum is, if you want to eat them, they've got to come back down eventually.

9

Vacuum released. SLOW MOTION — the whipped cream implodes, exploding outward from the force of the returning atmosphere. Dramatic, violent. Then the chocolate — still intact. The split screen collapses to the chocolate mold. A digital cutaway effect peels away the surface to reveal the internal honeycomb structure — thin crystalline walls surrounding air pockets. The structure that survived.

10

CHRIS in the airplane cabin. He picks up a chocolate bar from the tray. Snaps it. Pops a piece in his mouth.

CHRIS

The chocolate solidified and held up. But why does chocolate harden enough that it snaps when you break it?

He eats the piece.

CHRIS

And why does it melt so easily — as though it were never solid at all? What makes chocolate special?

11

STOCK FOOTAGE — airplane touching down. No voiceover.

12

CHRIS walks out of a tropical airport into heat and humidity. Palm trees.

CHRIS

The answer to that question is sixty-five million years old. And it starts here, in the tropics of Central America.

He gets into a taxi and it drives off. The camera watches it leave.

— The Tree —

13

JUNGLE B-ROLL. Dense canopy. Shafts of light filtering through. Humidity on the lens. Insects. Birdsong. Mud. Roots. The overwhelming density of the tropical lowlands. A few quick establishing cuts to build a sense of place.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Theobroma cacao — the chocolate tree — has been growing in jungles like this one for sixty-five million years. Since before the asteroid that killed the dinosaurs. It only grows within twenty degrees of the equator, in the understory of the tropical rainforest.

14

CHRIS and a LOCAL GUIDE move through the undergrowth. Pushing through vegetation. They arrive at a cacao tree. The pods are immediately strange — big, ridged, colorful, growing directly from the trunk, not the branches. The guide reaches up with a machete and cuts a pod from the trunk. Hands it to Chris. Chris holds it — it's heavy, alien-looking. He turns it in his hands for the camera.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The pods grow directly from the trunk — a trait called cauliflory. They have to, because the pods are so heavy they'd snap a branch.

15

The guide takes the pod back and cracks it open with the machete. Chris looks inside. CLOSE-UP — the inside is nothing like what the audience expects. White, slimy pulp surrounding rows of purple-brown beans. Chris reaches in, pulls out a bean, holds it up.

CHRIS

Inside every one of these pods — about forty beans, surrounded by this sweet white pulp.

16

B-ROLL — monkeys (ideally white-faced capuchins) interacting with cacao pods. Grabbing pods from the ground or off the trunk. Bashing them against a branch to crack them open. Eating the sweet white pulp. Spitting out or discarding the bitter seeds on the jungle floor.

Production: Option A — research and buy existing stock footage of capuchins with cacao pods. Option B — commission wildlife B-roll at a cacao farm in Costa Rica or Panama where capuchins are common (e.g., Hacienda Barú, Costa Rica). A wildlife camera operator near fruiting trees for a few days should capture usable footage.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The pods can't open on their own. The tree relies on animals — monkeys, rodents, birds — to crack them open. They're after the sweet pulp. That's the taxi fare for the seeds. The seeds themselves are so bitter the animals either spit them out or swallow them whole and poop them out undigested somewhere else in the jungle. Either way, the tree gets what it wants.

17

Back to Chris at the tree. He's holding the bean from shot 15.

CHRIS

But inside every one of these beans is a fat that is unlike anything else in nature.

18

Chris crouches near the base of the tree. Fallen pods, decomposing matter, warm soil. He picks up a seed from the ground.

CHRIS

And now this seed has a problem: to germinate, it needs energy. And that energy is stored as fat — inside the two halves of the seed, the cotyledons. Over half the weight of this bean is fat. Cocoa butter. But the soil here is always warm — seventy-five, eighty-five, ninety degrees.

19

B-ROLL — the jungle floor. Fallen leaves, decomposing pods, insects, heat shimmer. The hostile environment.

CHRIS (V.O.)

In this heat, a liquid oil breaks down quickly and loses its energy value.

20

Chris holds the seed, turning it in his hand.

CHRIS (V.O.)

But a fully solid fat — one that melts well above jungle temperatures — would be locked away. The seedling could never access it.

21

Chris handles a piece of raw cocoa butter — the pale, waxy fat extracted from the beans. It's solid in the shade. He holds it in his palm. It starts to soften and melt visibly.

CHRIS

This is cocoa butter — the fat inside the seed. Solid at soil temperature. But hold it in your hand and it melts. Not gradually — all at once. The seed is, essentially, a vault with a thermostat.

22

TIMELAPSE — a cacao seed germinating over 30 days, compressed into ~15 seconds. Practical timelapse with AI-assisted compositing to place it in the jungle environment. The root emerges first, pushing downward. The stem pushes up, lifting the two cotyledon halves above the soil. The cotyledons open and turn green. First true leaves unfurl. Reference: youtube.com/watch?v=mUYa8GtWSgA

CHRIS (V.O.)

As the seed germinates, the temperature rises slightly and the vault opens. The fat melts and fuels everything you're watching — the root, the stem, the first leaves. Thirty days of growth, powered by cocoa butter.

23

CAMERA PUSHES IN on a cacao bean or the cocoa butter in Chris's hand — closer and closer until we TRANSITION TO ANIMATED GRAPHICS, zooming into the molecular level.

CHRIS (V.O.)

But how does the tree build a fat with such a precise melting point?

ANIMATION: A fat molecule appears — the trident shape. Three fatty acid chains hanging off a glycerol backbone.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Every fat — whether it comes from a cow, an olive, a coconut, or a cacao pod — is built on the same scaffold. A backbone with three chains. A trident.

24

ANIMATION: Two types of chains side by side. A straight, rigid chain. A kinked chain with a fixed bend.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The chains come in two types. Straight — rigid, no bends. Or kinked — with a fixed angle that can't be straightened. Straight chains pack tightly together. That's a solid fat. Kinked chains can't pack — they push each other apart. That's a liquid oil.

25

ANIMATION: A jumble of differently-shaped tridents — random combinations of straight and kinked chains. They tumble, unable to organize. Labeled MOST FATS.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Most plants don't care which chains go where. They assemble their fats from whatever's available. The result is a random assortment — every trident a different shape. They can't organize. That's why butter softens gradually. Every molecule is in a different situation, held by different forces, letting go at a different temperature.

26

ANIMATION: An enzyme appears — a molecular machine. It selects specific chains and places them deliberately. Straight on position one. Kinked in the middle. Straight on position three. The same shape, over and over. The tridents emerging are all identical. Labeled COCOA BUTTER.

CHRIS (V.O.)

But the cacao tree has an unusually picky enzyme. Instead of grabbing whatever's available, it strongly prefers to put a straight chain on position one, a kinked chain in the middle, and a straight chain on position three. The same shape. Over and over. Eighty percent of cocoa butter is three molecules that are nearly identical.

27

ANIMATION: The identical tridents line up, organizing into a lattice. A crystal forming. Then — the crystal melts all at once, every molecule releasing simultaneously.

CHRIS (V.O.)

And when every molecule is the same shape, they can line up. They can organize. They can form a solid. And when that solid melts, every molecule lets go at the same temperature. All at once. That's the sharp melt. That's the thermostat.

28

ANIMATION TRANSITIONS BACK to the real world — pulling out from the molecular level back to Chris in the jungle. The cocoa butter melted in his palm.

CHRIS

A vault that opens at germination temperature. Sixty-five million years of evolution, solved by a picky enzyme.

29

Chris stands, looking out at the jungle. The seedling from the timelapse, or another young cacao sapling nearby.

CHRIS

This narrow temperature window — solid here, liquid just a few degrees warmer — is what makes cocoa butter extraordinary. It's what will give chocolate its snap and its melt. But it's also what makes chocolate one of the most temperamental materials in any kitchen. The same thermostat that lets this seed germinate is the reason tempering exists. And it's the reason my aerobar nearly drove me insane. But we'll get to that.

30

Brief convergent evolution beat — shea in West Africa, illipe in Borneo, sal in India. Different continents, different lineages, same answer.

CHRIS

Cacao isn't the only tree that evolved this trick. But it's the one that changed the world. Not because of its fat — because of what's inside these beans.

— The Bean —

31

Chris bites a raw, unfermented cacao bean. His face tells the story — bitter, astringent, tannic.

CHRIS

This is what chocolate tastes like before humans get involved. It's awful. Chocolate doesn't exist in nature. It had to be invented.

— The Drink —

32

The metate — stone grinding cacao. The frothy drink poured between vessels.

Through fermentation, roasting, grinding, sweetening — centuries of cultural innovation — chocolate was a drink for three thousand years.

CHRIS

The Spanish brought this drink to Europe. It became fashionable — chocolate houses across London and Paris. But in eighteenth-century England, there was another drink that was far more popular.

33

Historical engravings of the Thames — an open sewer. Cholera. Disease.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Water would kill you. It was contaminated. So people drank something else.

34

Chris sitting in a dark, historic English pub. Low ceilings, dark wood. He's holding a gin.

CHRIS

Gin was safer than water. Cheaper than beer. And England was drinking itself to death.

— The Quakers —

35

CHRIS

These people needed something to put in the cup.

CHRIS

They chose chocolate.

36

Map graphic — Bristol, Birmingham, York. Three Quaker families selling drinking chocolate as the moral alternative to gin.

CHRIS

All selling drinking chocolate. All competing. And all about to be changed by an accident in Holland.

— The Waste Product —

37

Chris holding cocoa powder in one hand, a block of pale cocoa butter in the other.

CHRIS

In 1828, a Dutchman named Van Houten invented a press to squeeze the fat out of ground cacao. He was trying to make a better drink — the powder dissolves more easily without the greasy fat. The cocoa butter was waste. Nobody wanted it.

38

Beat. Chris brings the two halves together.

CHRIS

Twenty years later, a Quaker named Fry had a counterintuitive idea. Put the waste back. Mix the fat with the powder and sugar. The result was a paste you could pour into a mold. The first solid chocolate bar. 1847.

CHRIS

The waste product IS the crystal. Everything in this story — the tree's evolution, the science, the tempering, the aerobar — depends on a fat that was once thrown away.

— The Chocolate Wars —

39

Quick montage — Bournville, New Earswick, archival advertising, factory images. The solid bar set off a war among the three Quaker families. Competition drove innovation but couldn't solve the fundamental problem: the chocolate was grainy, it didn't snap.

CHRIS

The chocolate still wasn't good enough. And the British couldn't fix it.

— The Aero Patent —

40

Chris in York. The Cocoa Works — now luxury apartments. Walking the streets.

Rowntree's was losing to Cadbury. 1935 — the Aero bar. Patent GB 459583. Materials science disguised as candy making.

CHRIS

The candy bar I bought at Heathrow was born right here. From Rowntree's desperation to compete with Cadbury. But nobody knew why it worked. And the British couldn't solve the deeper problem — the graininess, the lack of snap. That answer came from somewhere else.

— Switzerland —

41

Total pattern interrupt. Swiss Alps. Green pastures. Dairy cows. The Matterhorn.

CHRIS

Daniel Peter lived next door to Henri Nestlé. One made chocolate. The other made baby food. Together — 1875 — they invented milk chocolate.

42

Bern, Switzerland. Lindt's story. His original machine — granite roller in a granite trough.

CHRIS

Four years later, Rodolphe Lindt left his mixing machine running over a weekend. He came back on Monday to the best chocolate anyone had ever tasted. Smooth where it had been gritty. Glossy where it had been dull. Another accident. He had no idea what had happened.

43

Tobler and tempering. A Toblerone bar. The word "tempering" on screen.

CHRIS

Jean Tobler figured out tempering. Glossy. Snappy. Stable. But he didn't know why it worked. Nobody did. And the word he chose came from a completely different craft.

44

CHRIS

A few years before Lindt's accident, a young telegraph operator was doing odd jobs in Cincinnati. James Gamble — co-founder of a small candle and soap company — personally hired him to build a communication device between the office and the factory.

Beat.

CHRIS

The young man's name was Thomas Edison.

An old-fashioned Edison bulb flickers on. A candle goes out.

CHRIS

1879. The same year a lightbulb went off for Lindt in Switzerland, Edison literally invented the lightbulb in America. And it was about to extinguish the candle business for his former employers — William Procter and James Gamble.

— The Soap Company —

46

A butcher hands Chris a slab of fat.

CHRIS

Cincinnati, Ohio. 1837. Porkopolis. An English candlemaker and an Irish soapmaker marry a pair of sisters and go into business together. Their raw material? Pig fat.

47

1880s — Soap. Candle business dead. P&G pivots. Soap needs fat. Meatpackers control the price.

48

1890s — Cottonseed. Waste from the cotton gin. Cheap and abundant. But it's liquid.

49

1907 — Hydrogenation. Fat molecules are tridents. Straighten the kinks, liquid becomes solid.

CHRIS

This is alchemy. For the first time, humans can engineer fat.

50

Chris holding a can of Crisco.

CHRIS

Crisco. CRYStalized Cottonseed Oil. The crystal is right there in the name.

51

1912 — Munich. Von Laue fires X-rays at a crystal. X-ray crystallography.

52

Chris holding a print of Photo 51.

CHRIS

1952. King's College London. Rosalind Franklin exposes DNA to X-rays for a hundred hours. Photo 51. The most important X-ray image ever taken. Watson and Crick used it — without her knowledge — to build the double helix. They won the Nobel. She died at thirty-seven. But the technique was proven. Point X-rays at any crystal and read its structure.

53

Chris picks up a cocoa butter diffraction image.

CHRIS

1966. Cincinnati. Two scientists at P&G point X-rays at cocoa butter. Not because they care about chocolate. Because they care about fat. Six crystal structures. Six polymorphs. The same technique that found the secret of life found the secret of chocolate.

— The Crystal —

54

Chris holds up bloomed chocolate — white, chalky. Next to a tempered piece — glossy, dark. He snaps the good one.

CHRIS

You've seen this. You threw it away. It hasn't gone bad. What you're looking at is a crystal. The wrong crystal.

55

The cacao tree's enzyme sorts fat molecules: straight, kinked, straight. Eighty percent identical. Uniform molecules can form crystals — six arrangements, six forms. Only Form V gives the snap.

CHRIS

One crystal out of six. And tempering is how you choose it.

— The Bladesmith —

56

Close-up: stone grinding steel. Sparks. Pull back to reveal a forge.

CHRIS

"Tempering" is a metallurgy word. It's the same tool — erasing crystal structure with heat, selecting the right one through controlled cooling. Same physics. Different material.

57

The bladesmith tempers a blade — steel glowing, quenching, controlled cooling.

The grinding stone connection: metate → conche → whetstone.

CHRIS

The stone that makes chocolate smooth is the same stone that makes a blade sharp.

— Spinnaker —

58

Chris arrives at Spinnaker Chocolate, Seattle. Small space, homemade equipment. Meets Chris and Kelly Van Arsdale. Two ingredients. No shortcuts. Sorting, roasting, grinding, conching. The conche — granite wheels descended from the Mesoamerican metate.

CHRIS

The chocolate comes off the conche smooth. But it still needs to be tempered.

— Simple Tempering —

59

A microwave. A microplane. A digital thermometer. And some chocolate.

CHRIS

Let me show you how to get chocolate to pack correctly. You'll need a microwave, a microplane, a digital thermometer... and some chocolate.

60

Step 1: Erase crystal memory. Melt to ~120°F.
Step 2: Cool to ~82°F.
Step 3: Seed with microplaned tempered chocolate. Form V templates.
Step 4: Rewarm to ~88-90°F. Only Form V survives.

Pour, cool, set. Glossy. Snappy. Chris snaps the finished chocolate. Clean crack.

CHRIS

A microwave, a microplane, and a thermometer. That's it.

Beat.

CHRIS

But the aerobars... the aerobars are somewhat more involved.

— The Descent Into Madness —

61

The shells — molds lined with tempered chocolate. A clock starts. Five minutes of working time.

62

The filling — tempered separately, loaded into a pressurized metal siphon with nitrous oxide.

63

The exploding siphon — war story. Chocolate clogged. He forced it. Pressurized chocolate everywhere.

64

Filling the shells — foams as it exits the siphon. Get it in before it sets.

65

The vacuum chamber — bubbles expand. Too far and walls collapse. No stop button. Chris pulled the power cord.

CHRIS

Practice ahead of theory. One last time.

66

The wait — near-vacuum. Radiative cooling only. Very slow.

67

Pressure restored. Flex the molds. The aerobars release. Mirror surface. Impossibly light. Honeycomb of crystal.

68

CHRIS

I can't tell you how many times it didn't work. Collapsed bubbles. Melted shells. Exploded siphons. Chocolate on the ceiling. Months of starting over. But the first time it came out of the chamber and I flexed the mold and it released — with that surface, that weight, that sound when you tap it — I knew. Everything I'd learned about crystals, about pressure, about fat and temperature and time — it was all in there. In a piece of chocolate I could hold in my hand.

— Closing —

69

Chris is back where the film began. The Fat Duck dining room. The mandarin aerated chocolate arrives — the actual dish, plated. He looks at it.

CHRIS

Twenty-three years ago, I bought a candy bar at an airport. I didn't know it was the latest accident in a chain that goes back sixty-five million years.

A tropical tree that solved a germination problem by evolving the only fat on earth that could form a crystal. A Mesoamerican drink ground on a stone. Contaminated water that made England drink gin. Quakers who needed something to put in the cup. A Dutchman who threw away the most valuable part of the bean. A Quaker who put it back. Three families at war. A Swiss man who forgot to turn off his machine. His neighbor who made baby food. A young telegraph operator who killed his former employer's candle business with a lightbulb. A soap company that spent sixty years pivoting from pig fat to the science of crystals. And a cook who bought a candy bar at Heathrow because he'd never seen one before.

CHRIS

That candy bar in the airport. This is what it became.

71

CHRIS

But if you just want to temper chocolate at home — a microwave, a microplane, and a thermometer. You don't need twenty years.
